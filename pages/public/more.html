COVID-19

History

The first reported case and the initial reaction to COVID-19

The World Health Organization (WHO) was notified of cases of pneumonia in Wuhan, China, with no known cause on December 31, 2019, marking the first official cases of COVID-19. China's authorities identified a novel coronavirus, 2019-nCoV, as the cause of these illnesses on January 7th.

On the 30th of January 2020, the WHO classified the fast expanding COVID-19 outbreak a Public Health Emergency of International Concern. However, it wasn't until the 11th of February the following month that the novel coronavirus was given an official name: COVID-19. The US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) confirmed the first COVID-19 death in the country nine days later. The individual was a Washington state resident in his fifties.

Declaring COVID-19 a pandemic

COVID-19 began with global health authorities, government agencies, and the general public apprehensive of how the disease would spread and effect daily life. The United Nations released $15 million on March 1st, 2020 to help the worldwide COVID-19 response. COVID-19 instances surpassed 100,000 a week later, on March 7th. COVID-19 was declared a pandemic by the WHO several days later, on March 11th. COVID-19 went from a serious concern that seemed to be exclusive to China to a global health emergency practically quickly.

Following the implementation of exceptional steps to contain the virus, the situation in Wuhan had calmed down by this time. China was reporting thousands of new cases each day at the start of the outbreak, but by March, that number had dropped to a few dozens. Cases in Europe, on the other hand, were fast increasing day by day, with Italy registering an astonishing 250 deaths between March 12th and March 13th. As a result, the WHO determined on March 13th that Europe had become the pandemic's epicenter. The United States proclaimed a state of emergency on the same day.

Variants

New variants change the course of the pandemic.

Due of the constraints their people had to face to prevent the spread of the virus, many countries observed a decline in cases, hospitalizations, and fatalities over the summer. The Lambda variety was first identified in Peru near the conclusion of the summer, in August 2020. According to the WHO, this variety has now spread to at least 29 nations.

The Alpha version was first discovered in the UK a month later, in September 2020. These variants' identification was crucial since it indicated that the virus was changing. As a result, disease symptoms and outcomes were shifting. For example, evidence suggests that the Alpha variation may increase the chance of poor COVID-19 outcomes.

With the introduction of these new varieties, COVID-19 cases began to grow again in many nations, and by September 29, 2020, 1 million COVID-19 deaths had occurred.

Vaccines

Vaccines imitate the virus or bacteria that causes sickness and cause the body to produce antibodies in response. Once a person is infected with the disease-causing virus or bacteria, these antibodies will defend them.

WHO has determined that the following vaccines against COVID-19 meet the relevant safety and effectiveness requirements as of April 8, 2022:

  • AstraZeneca/Oxford vaccine
  • Johnson and Johnson
  • Moderna
  • Pfizer/BionTech
  • Sinopharm
  • Sinovac
  • COVAXIN
  • Covovax
  • Nuvaxovid
  • CanSino

Health Protocols

Get vaccinated and keep your COVID-19 vaccines up to date.

  • COVID-19 immunizations work well to keep you from getting sick. COVID-19 vaccines are extremely effective in averting serious disease, hospitalization, and death.
  • The greatest method to reduce the spread of SARS-CoV-2, the virus that causes COVID-19, is to be vaccinated.
  • The CDC recommends that everyone who is eligible, especially persons with weaker immune systems, get their COVID-19 immunizations.

Wear a mask

  • Regardless of immunization status, everyone over the age of two should wear a well-fitting mask indoors in public in locations where the COVID-19 Community Level is high.
  • Wear a mask that is tailored to your needs in terms of fit, protection, and comfort.
  • Wear a mask indoors in public if you're over the age of 2 and live in an area with a high COVID-19 Community Level.
  • Wear a mask if you're unwell and need to be among people, or if you're caring for someone with COVID-19.
  • Speak to your healthcare practitioner about wearing a mask at medium COVID-19 Community Levels if you are at an elevated risk of severe illness, or if you live with or spend time with someone who is.
  • Even if they are up to date on their COVID-19 immunizations, people who have an illness or are using medications that weaken their immune system may not be fully protected. They should speak with their doctors about any additional measures that may be required.

In enclosed areas of public transportation (such as airplanes, trains, buses, ferries) and transportation hubs (such as airports, stations, and seaports), everyone aged 2 and up, including passengers and workers, should wear a well-fitting mask or respirator, especially in crowded or poorly ventilated areas such as airport jetways.

Stay 6 feet away from others

  • If at all possible, avoid close contact with sick persons inside your home. Maintain a 6-foot distance between the sick person and other family members if at all possible. If you are caring for someone who is unwell, be sure you are wearing a well-fitting mask and following other precautions.
  • If you are not up to date on COVID-19 vaccines and are in a public setting, keep at least 6 feet away from other people, especially if you are at a higher risk of becoming severely sick from COVID-19.

Avoid crowded places with poor ventilation.

  • If possible, introduce fresh air into the house by opening windows and doors.
  • Avoid crowded areas and indoor spaces that do not have fresh air from the outdoors if you are at risk of becoming very sick from COVID-19.

Test to prevent spread to others

  • There are numerous types of examinations from which to pick.
  • SARS-CoV-2 (the virus that produces COVID-19) tests determine whether you are infected at the time of the test. Because it checks for viral infection, this type of test is termed a viral test.
  • A positive test result, regardless of the test type, indicates that you have an infection and that you should isolate and tell your close contacts to avoid transmitting sickness to others.
  • Self-testing kits are viral tests that may be used at home or anywhere, are simple to use, and yield quick results. Self-tests can be used by anyone, regardless of their vaccination status or if they are experiencing symptoms.
  • COVID-19 self-tests are one of many risk-reduction techniques that safeguard you and others by minimizing the likelihood of COVID-19 spreading, such as immunization, masking, and physical separation.

Hands should be washed frequently.

  • After being in a public place, or after blowing your nose, coughing, or sneezing, wash your hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds.

Cover coughs and sneezes

  • You can cough or sneeze into your mask if you're wearing one. As quickly as possible, put on a fresh, clean mask and wash your hands.

Disinfect and clean

  • Clean high-touch surfaces on a regular basis or as needed, and after visitors. Tables, doorknobs, light switches, countertops, handles, desks, phones, keyboards, toilets, faucets, and sinks all fall under this category.
  • Disinfect commonly touched surfaces if someone is unwell or has tested positive for COVID-19.

Maintain a daily health check.

  • Observe the following signs and symptoms.
  • If you're running errands, going into the office or business, or in other situations where keeping a physical distance of 6 feet is impossible, keep an eye on your symptoms.
